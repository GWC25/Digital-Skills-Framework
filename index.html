Framing Digital Skills
for Industry Needs

How digital skills are framed determines whether staff and learners engage or disengage. The most effective framing connects directly to an industry outcome learners already care about. Below are four strategic approaches, each with worked examples across Weston College's curriculum areas.

Framing Approach 1

The Industry Standard Frame

Frame digital skills as standard professional practice — not technology training, but what every employer expects on day one.

Works best with learners resistant to "digital lessons" but responsive to employer expectations and industry norms.

Construction (CON/BUI)"Every major contractor uses digital clock-in. You won't get paid if you can't sign in on site digitally."

Engineering (EMV)"EV diagnostics are entirely digital — you can't fix a modern vehicle without a tablet."
Framing Approach 2

The Time-Saving Frame

Frame digital tools as reducing admin burden for tasks learners will have to do anyway — invoicing, booking, recording, communicating.

Effective for vocational learners who see admin as a burden and are motivated by efficiency and getting on with the job.

Hair & Beauty (HBH)"Fresha sends reminders automatically and takes deposits. You spend less time chasing no-shows."

Health & Care (HAC)"Electronic records mean you don't rewrite the same patient information by hand three times a shift."
Framing Approach 3

The Employability & Earnings Frame

Link digital competence directly to higher pay, better job prospects, or self-employment viability. Use Labour Market Intelligence to make the case concrete.

Most effective in sectors with visible wage differentials and high self-employment rates (beauty, creative, construction).

Business (BUS)"Xero certification adds £3-5k to your starting salary as a bookkeeper."

Performing Arts (PAP)"Casting directors now expect digital self-tapes. You won't get seen without knowing how to film and upload professionally."
Framing Approach 4

The Authentic Context Frame

Teach digital skills inside real vocational tasks rather than as separate lessons. The digital skill becomes invisible — the learner is just doing their job.

The most powerful approach for learners with low digital confidence or negative prior experiences. The learning happens because the task is meaningful.

Construction (CON)"Measure this wall, log the data, and email it to the project manager." — learner has just done data entry, file handling, and email in one task.

ESOL (ESO)"Book a GP appointment on the NHS app." — learner is navigating a real digital service, not a classroom simulation.
Strategic Principle

The Golden Rule of Industry Framing

Never teach the technology. Teach the task that technology enables.

A construction learner doesn't need to learn "how to use an app." They need to clock in, submit a site diary, order materials, and read a drawing. The apps are just how they do it. When digital skills are framed this way — across every curriculum area from Animal Management to Sixth Form — they stop being a separate burden and become invisible, purposeful, and self-evidently necessary.

SEND, Inclusion & Accessibility

Weston College is one of three SEND Centres for Excellence in the UK. Digital skills development for learners with special educational needs and disabilities requires a fundamentally different approach — technology here is not just a learning tool, it is often the means by which learning is possible at all. This section covers digital accessibility considerations for each FIP area, plus broader inclusion principles for all areas.

Connecting to strategy: This tab directly supports the Inclusion & Accessibility strategic pillar. Content here links to the Digital Accessibility for SEND learners Strategy Dossier and the Assistive Technology workstream with Lois Knight and Liz.

🔵 Autism (FAU)

Digital strengths & adjustments

  • Many autistic learners have strong digital aptitude — leverage this as a strength
  • Predictable, consistent digital environments reduce anxiety (consistent Teams layout, clear file naming)
  • AAC apps (Proloquo2Go, Grid 3) support communication in digital learning contexts
  • Visual schedule apps support session structure and transitions
  • Avoid unexpected platform changes without notice
  • Sensory considerations — screen brightness, sound alerts in digital tools

🟣 Complex Needs (FCO)

Enabling technology

  • Eye-gaze technology (Tobii) enables digital access for learners without motor control
  • Switch-access devices provide alternative computer interaction
  • Clicker / Widgit Online for literacy and communication support
  • Sensory interactive digital surfaces for engagement
  • Technology is often the primary communication channel — reliability is critical
  • Staff training on assistive technology is non-negotiable, not optional

🟢 Pre-Vocational / FPL

Digital for independence

  • Basic device operation as a foundation skill for transition to employment
  • Taskmaster / visual timetable apps support daily digital routines
  • Read&Write (text-to-speech, dictation) reduces writing barriers
  • Digital daily living tools: travel apps, banking apps, NHS App
  • Simple photo/video for portfolio and communication
  • Immersive Reader for accessible reading of digital content

🔴 ESOL Learners (ESO)

Language & digital barriers

  • Compound barrier: language AND digital exclusion simultaneously
  • Microsoft Translator and Google Translate as scaffolding tools (not dependency)
  • Real-world digital tasks (NHS App, DWP portal) are most motivating
  • Digital inclusion is societal inclusion for this cohort
  • Screen readers and audio features support language acquisition alongside digital skills
  • DfE data: ESOL = most popular essential skills subject (Q1 2025/26)

🟡 Dyslexia & SpLD

Across all areas

  • Read&Write Gold for text-to-speech, word prediction, screen masking
  • Immersive Reader built into Microsoft Teams and Word
  • OneNote digital inking for spatial note-taking
  • Dragon Dictate / speech-to-text for extended writing tasks
  • Digital coloured overlays / background colour in reading tools
  • AI summarisation tools to reduce reading demands without bypassing learning

🟠 Universal Design Principles

For all digital provision

  • WCAG 2.2 AA compliance for all digital resources created by staff
  • Captions on all video content (auto-generate then check in Teams/SharePoint)
  • Alt-text on all images in presentations and SharePoint pages
  • Accessible colour contrast in PowerPoints and digital materials
  • Multiple means of engagement — not just text, not just video
  • Check Accessibility tool in Microsoft Office — build this into staff CPD
Key Principle

Accessibility is not a Bolt-On

For Weston College, SEND and digital accessibility are part of the same conversation. The college's national recognition as a SEND Centre for Excellence means the standard of digital accessibility — in resources, environments, and staff knowledge — should be sector-leading, not just compliant. The best digital accessibility practice benefits all learners: captions help noisy environments, clear navigation helps anxious learners, and accessible colour contrast helps poor lighting conditions.
